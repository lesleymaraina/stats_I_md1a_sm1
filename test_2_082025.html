Introduction

In biomedical and clinical science, knowledge and insights emerge from laboratory experiments, clinical trials, population studies, or genomic databases, the conclusions we draw are anchored in observed data

  • the data often require interpretation:
    • determining whether a treatment effect is meaningful
    • whether a biomarker signal is reproducible
    • whether a genomic variant is rare or common
    • whether an observed association reflects biology rather than study design
  • statistics provides the framework for this interpretation and allows researchers to understand, evaluate, and generate biomedical evidence in a systematic way

Statistics and Scientific Literature

In scientific literature [i.e.: Science, Cell, Nature] readers often encounter metrics such as:

effect sizes, risk ratios, regression coefficients, confidence intervals, survival curves

  • metrics that express strength of evidence between associations and uncertainty

  • statistics in biomedicine provide the tools for distinguishing meaningful biological signals from background variation

Statistics and Clincial Science

  • study design decisions shape the type and strength of evidence produced
  • Example study design decisions:
    • cohort selection
    • measurement strategies
    • analytical approaches
  • statistical frameworks guide these decisions by clarifying how data can support specific scientific questions and how uncertainty will be quantified
  • designing a clinical trial involves anticipating variability and determining sample size requirements
  • designing a biomarker study involves assessing measurement reliability and effect magnitude
  • statistical approaches help define these choices

Statistics and Biomedical Science

Biomedical research often begins with a question that invovles determining:

What does our select dataset tell us about a broader population?

Scientific task generates numbers and our goal is to interpret those numbers as evidence

Approach for analysis would involve describing:

  • the scientific target of interest
  • the observed data used as evidence
  • the quantity the analysis aims to estimate
  • the uncertainty that accompanies any conclusion

Core Statistical Definitions

  • define terms within the basic framework for statistical inference, regression modeling, and biomedical decision-making

  • population: complete set of biological units, measurements, or possible outcomes relevant to a scientific question

  • population is often entirely unobservable

Examples

  • all patients with pancreatic cancer in the United States

  • all circulating tumor cells in a single patient’s bloodstream

  • all potential gene expression profiles under a defined biological condition

  • statistical test exist because we cannot measure every unit in the population; instead, we draw inferences from samples

Sample: Observable Subset of the Population

Sample

  • subset of the population that we actually observe and measure
  • sample: data available for analysis
  • statistical conclusions originate from this subset

Statistical inference answers:

What does this sample tell us about the population?

Examples:

  • 200 patients enrolled in a clinical trial
  • 50 tumor biopsies selected for sequencing
  • 10,000 cells measured in single‑cell RNA‑seq

Random Sampling and Statistical Inference

  • random sampling: a data collection process in which each unit in the target population has a known, non-zero probability of selection determined by chance rather than investigator choice

Random sampling helps ensure:

  • absence of systematic selection bias
  • representation of population heterogeneity
  • quantifiable sampling uncertainty
  • validity of probability-based inference

Consequence of non-random sampling

  • estimates may be biased
  • variability cannot be quantified correctly
  • population inference may be invalid

Sampling Variability and Behavior of Sample Statistics

Sampling variability: - inherent variation in a statistic across repeated random samples from the same population - observable consequence of random sampling and represents the distribution of possible estimates that could have been obtained under repeated data collection

  • sampling variability is linked to:

    • confidence intervals \(\rightarrow\) quantifying estimation uncertainty
    • hypothesis testing \(\rightarrow\) assessing compatibility with models
    • regression inference \(\rightarrow\) evaluating parameter uncertainty

Statistics and Variation Across Samples

  • sampling variability: variation in a statistic across repeated samples drawn from the same population

  • value reflects the randomness inherent in sampling rather than true differences in the underlying population

  • scenario: repeat a study and collect a new sample

  • outcome: obtain a different statistic

Example:

Study 1: response rate = 42%
Study 2: response rate = 47%

Statistical Inference Requires Sampling

Measuring the entire population is typically impossible due to: - Cost - Time - Ethical constraints - Biological feasibility

Examples:

  • Cannot sequence every cancer cell in a tumor

  • Cannot enroll every patient in a clinical trial

  • Cannot observe every biological realization

  • sampling makes statistical inference feasible

Parameter: Linking Population to Sample

Parameter: fixed but unknown numerical characteristic of the population

Examples:

  • True cancer prevalence rate
  • True mean gene expression level
  • True treatment response probability

Nomenclature examples:

\[ \mu, \; \sigma, \; p, \; \beta \]

Statistic: Observable Estimate of the Parameter

  • statistic: computed from the sample and used to estimate the parameter

  • statistics: random variables because they depend on the sampled data

Examples:

  • Sample mean: \(\bar{x}\) estimates population mean \(\mu\)
  • Sample proportion: \(\hat{p}\) estimates population proportion \(p\)
  • Regression coefficients estimate population relationships

Definition: Statistic

  • statistic: numerical summary computed from a sample

  • a statistic describes what we observed in the data

Examples:

  • Sample mean age of patients: 63.4 years

  • Sample response rate: 42%

  • Observed allele frequency in gnomAD: 0.003

  • Note: these numbers summarize the sample — not the entire population

Statistic vs Parameter

parameter:

  • fixed numerical characteristic of the entire population
  • value does not change
  • value typically cannot observed directly

Example: true mean gene expression under a condition \(\mu\)

statistic:

  • numerical summary computed from sample data
  • value can vary from sample to sample
  • serves as an estimate of the corresponding parameter

Example: sample mean gene expression under a condition \(\bar{\mu}\)

Note:

  • never directly observe parameters
  • observe statistics \(\rightarrow\) use probability theory to understand how those statistics relate to the underlying parameters

Example: Biomedical Example: Sampling Variability in Population Genomics

Background: What is allele frequency?

  • allele frequency: proportion of all gene copies at a specific genomic locus within a defined population that carry a particular allele
  • allele frequency: reflects how common that genetic variant is in that population

Allele frequency is therefore calculated as:

\[ \text{Allele frequency} = \frac{\text{Number of occurrences of a given allele}} {\text{Total number of alleles at that locus in the population}} \]

Example: Biomedical Example: Sampling Variability in Population Genomics

Population parameter

  • true allele frequency of a pathogenic variant among all humans
  • represents a biological property of the population
  • fixed but unknown because sequencing every human is infeasible

Statistic

  • observed allele frequency calculated from a sample dataset [i.e.:gnomAD ]
  • population databases [i.e.: gnomAD] are large but finite samples
  • serves as an estimate of the population parameter
  • value depends on which individuals were included in the sample

Conceptual Model of Statistical Inference

  • statistical inference is a process that uses observed data to learn about unknown features of a population

  • translates sample-derived summaries into probabilistic statements about population parameters while accounting for sampling variability

Workflow:

Population (unknown parameter) \(\rightarrow\) Random sampling \(\rightarrow\) Observed sample \(\rightarrow\) Compute statistic \(\rightarrow\) Estimate population parameter \(\rightarrow\) Quantify uncertainty

Concept Review

  • population:
    • population contains the biological or clinical quantity of interest — such as a response probability, allele frequency, or survival rate
    • quantity is a parameter \(\rightarrow\) fixed but unknown
  • random sampling:
    • subset of units [patients, genomes, cells] selected from the population
    • sampling introduces randomness \(\rightarrow\) only some population members are observed
  • observed sample: collected dataset represents one realization from many possible samples that could have been obtained

Concept Review

  • compute statistic:statistic [i.e.: proportion, regression coefficient, hazard ratio] summarizes information in the sample

  • estimate population parameter:

    • statistic serves as an estimate of the population parameter
    • evidence-based approximation of the underlying biological quantity

  • quantify uncertainty:

    • statistic depends on the sample, it is subject to sampling variability
    • statistical inference therefore quantifies uncertainty using tools such as standard errors, confidence intervals, likelihood, or posterior distributions
